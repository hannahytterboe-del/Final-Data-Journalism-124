# ***Park With Caution: The Five Most Common San Francisco Parking Violations of 2026***

*By Hannah Ytterboe*

In the past six months, San Francisco has seen 623,271 parking violations, causing drivers to pay the city a total of just more than *$69 million*. 

Of these 106 types of violations found by the San Francisco Municipal Transportation Agency, five stood out as the most common. According to an analysis of data compiled between the dates of Dec. 26, 2025 and June 26, 2026, parking a vehicle on the street during the timeframe of street cleaning was the most common with a total of 238,222 incidents, costing drivers *$25,012,768*. On their data collection service site, SFMTA gives each violation a brief code, pertaining to this violation it is STR CLEAN.

The second most common violation found in the data analysis was vehicles parked past the posted time limit of a meter, with a violation code of MTR OUT DT. It cost drivers *$8,070,690* during this time period. 

The third most common violation consisted of vehicles being parked for longer than the posted time limit in a Residential Parking Permit zone without displaying a valid permit for that specific neighborhood zone, costing drivers *$5,219,640*. The corresponding violation code for this is RES/OT.

The fourth most common violation, pertained to vehicles being parked at a parking meter in San Francisco's designated Downtown Core where the time on the meter had either expired or was not paid at all during enforcement hours, has violation code of METER DTN. This cost drivers a total of *$2,391,335*.

The fifth most common violation, for vehicles being parked or left standing on a public street without both front and rear license plates attached, cost drivers a total of *$2,421,071*. The corresponding code for this is NO PLATES.

## **Data Analysis Methods**

The data was downloaded to and analyzed on the attached Google Sheet.   
[View the Project Data on Google Sheets.](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/1VIeJQQEBlnIxDNrET7fDyvvV16QHmINO/edit?gid=261316779#gid=261316779)

I got the data set from the San Francisco Open Data Portal using the specific data set SFMTA \- Parking Citations & Fines. This data set is derived from the SFMTA internal management system. It aggregates daily records of all transit and parking citations issued by the SFMTA, the Sheriff’s Department, and the San Francisco Police Department. SFMTA is a government agency. The source does not appear to have a political agenda since it is derived straight from parking citations and fines. This data set is not necessarily academically trustworthy just because it is public and derived from government entities but more so because it was compiled from recorded parking citations and fines. Something that the dataset is overlooking is unrecorded violations due to its compiling being only of recorded citations and fines. 

To calculate the amount of money paid in fines per violation type, a pivot table in Google Sheets was created. The violation categories were set as rows and the amount of money fined was set as the values and counted as SUM in order to calculate the total sum of each category. 

[The pivot table can be viewed on the attached Google Sheet.](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/1VIeJQQEBlnIxDNrET7fDyvvV16QHmINO/edit?gid=261316779#gid=261316779)

36.22% of the total fees in the six-month period come from street cleaning violations. Out of 623,264 total parking violations, parking during street cleaning reached 238,222 occurrences, amounting to 38.22% of all parking violations during the six-month period. This number is more than three times higher than the second most common violation occurring at 12.1%. 

The third most common violation accounts for about 7.75% of all violations in the city during the six-month period, which is just under 20% the amount of street cleaning violations. Interestingly, even though METER DTN was the fourth most common violation type, it was the fifth most expensive in terms of the total sum of that violation. NO PLATES had a more expensive total sum for its violation than METER DTN, even though it occurred less than METER DTN violations. 

## **The Ethics of Parking Ticket Collection Reporting**

Overall, street cleaning violations were the most common of all 106 violation types. They made up 38.22% of total tickets and 36.22% of total revenue during the six-month period. 

The data itself does not have misrepresentation of communities since it came straight from the parking and transit citations. However, it does raise the question of if who gave out the tickets misrepresented any communities. In order to investigate this, interviews with anecdotal evidence would have to be conducted to see if there was misrepresentation among certain communities.
